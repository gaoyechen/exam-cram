# Meta-Skills: Universal Exam Tactics

Not knowledge. Not theory. Pure battlefield tactics for passing exams you haven't properly studied for.

---

## 1. Multiple Choice Mastery

### 1.1 Elimination Method (排除法)

The single most powerful exam technique. Instead of finding the right answer, eliminate wrong ones.

**Process:**
1. Read all 4 options before doing anything
2. Eliminate options that are clearly wrong (usually 1-2)
3. Among remaining options, look for these patterns:
   - **Extreme language** ("always", "never", "all", "none") — usually wrong
   - **Hedging language** ("usually", "generally", "most", "often") — usually right
   - **Longest option** — often correct (test writers need precision for right answers)
   - **Most specific option** — often correct (vague options are usually distractors)

**Advanced elimination:**
- If two options say the same thing in different words, both are wrong
- If two options are opposites, one of them is usually right
- If one option contradicts the premise of the question, eliminate it
- If one option uses unfamiliar/complex terminology while others use simple words, the complex one is often wrong (distractors use jargon to seem plausible)

### 1.2 Keyword Recognition (关键词识别)

Every question has signal words that point to the answer.

**Question signal words:**
- "EXCEPT" / "NOT" / "INCORRECT" — reverse your logic, you're looking for the wrong answer
- "BEST" / "MOST" — multiple options may be partially correct, pick the most complete one
- "FIRST" / "PRIMARY" — look for the most fundamental or initial step
- "MOST LIKELY" — probability-based, pick the safest/most common answer

**Answer signal words:**
- "All of the above" — if you're sure two options are correct, pick this
- "None of the above" — risky, usually avoid unless you're certain
- Options with numbers/statistics — verify the number matches what you know

### 1.3 Smart Guessing (智能猜测)

When you genuinely don't know the answer, maximize your odds.

**Evidence-based strategies:**
- "All of the above" is correct ~60-70% of the time when it appears (this is the best-documented pattern)
- Pick the option you've seen most often in your (limited) study
- Pick the option that "sounds like a textbook" — formal language signals correctness
- If one option is significantly different from the other three in structure/length, it's often the answer
- When truly stuck between two options, go with your first instinct

**⚠️ Important caveat:** The old advice that "B and C are more often correct" is NOT reliably true across different exams. Don't rely on letter-position patterns — they vary by exam and can change between test versions. Instead, focus on content-based elimination.

### 1.4 Option Comparison (选项对比)

Don't evaluate each option in isolation — compare them to each other.

**Technique:**
1. Group similar options together (e.g., A and C both say "increase", B and D say "decrease")
2. First decide which group is correct
3. Then narrow within the group
4. This reduces a 4-choice problem to two 2-choice problems

### 1.5 The "Two-Pass Mark" System (两轮标记法)

On your first pass through the exam:
- **No mark** = confident answer
- **? mark** = narrowed to 2 options, need to revisit
- **?? mark** = no idea at all

This helps you allocate your second-pass time efficiently — spend it on ? marks first (50% chance) before ?? marks (25% chance).

## 2. Short Answer / Essay Tactics

### 2.1 Keyword Harvesting (关键词踩点)

Graders scan for specific keywords, not beautiful prose.

**Process:**
1. Read the question, identify the core topic
2. List 3-5 keywords/concepts related to that topic
3. Write a sentence containing each keyword
4. Connect sentences into a paragraph
5. Bold or underline keywords if format allows

**Template formula:**
> [Topic] is defined as [keyword 1]. The key characteristics include [keyword 2] and [keyword 3]. This is important because [keyword 4]. For example, [keyword 5].

### 2.2 Template-Based Answers (模板化答题)

For common question types, use pre-built answer templates.

**"Compare and contrast" template:**
> Both [A] and [B] share [similarity]. However, they differ in [difference 1] and [difference 2]. [A] is characterized by [feature], while [B] is characterized by [feature]. The key distinction is [main difference].

**"Explain the process" template:**
> The process of [topic] involves [number] steps. First, [step 1]. Then, [step 2]. Finally, [step 3]. The outcome is [result].

**"Analyze and evaluate" template:**
> [Topic] has both advantages and disadvantages. On one hand, [advantage 1] and [advantage 2]. On the other hand, [disadvantage 1] and [disadvantage 2]. Overall, [balanced judgment with reasoning].

**"Cause and effect" template (新增):**
> The fundamental cause of [phenomenon] is [root cause]. This leads to [direct effect 1], which in turn causes [secondary effect 2]. The ultimate result is [final outcome].

### 2.3 Partial Credit Strategy (分步得分)

Even if you don't know the complete answer, maximize partial credit.

**Rules:**
- Always write something — blank = 0, wrong = sometimes partial credit
- Start with what you know, even if incomplete
- Use "buzzwords" from the course material
- If the question has multiple parts, answer each part separately
- Write more rather than less — graders look for correct points, not penalize wrong ones (usually)
- **Number your points** — makes it easier for graders to find your keywords

### 2.4 The "Brain Dump" Opening (开场倾倒法)

When you see a question and your mind goes blank:

1. Write down EVERYTHING you remember about the topic — unstructured, stream of consciousness
2. Look at what you wrote — keywords and connections will emerge
3. Organize the raw material into a structured answer
4. This works because recall is associative — writing one thing triggers another

## 3. Practical / Hands-On Exam Tactics

### 3.1 Do What You Know First (先做会的)

In practical exams, confidence compounds.

**Strategy:**
1. Quick scan of all questions (2 minutes)
2. Start with the one you're most confident about
3. Build momentum and confidence
4. Return to harder ones with remaining time
5. Never spend more than 30% of total time on a single question

### 3.2 Step-by-Step Scoring (分步得分)

Practical exams usually grade steps, not just final results.

**Approach:**
- Show every step, even if the final answer is wrong
- If you make an error early, continue with the correct process from that point
- Write down your assumptions explicitly — graders may give credit for valid assumptions
- If you can't complete a task, document what you would do next

### 3.3 Check Strategy (检查策略)

Don't just re-read your answers — actively verify.

**Priority order:**
1. Check questions worth the most points first
2. Check questions you were unsure about
3. Verify calculations by working backwards
4. For code: trace through with a sample input
5. For essays: scan for the keywords you intended to include

### 3.4 The "Save State" Rule (存档法则)

For computer-based practical exams:

- **Save early, save often** — don't lose work to crashes
- **Before any risky operation**, note what you're about to do so you can undo
- **If the exam allows multiple attempts** on a question, attempt it twice with different approaches rather than spending all your time perfecting one
- **Test incrementally** — don't build the whole thing then test; test each piece as you go

## 4. Time Management

### 4.1 The 3-Pass Strategy (三遍法)

Never get stuck on one question.

**Pass 1 (40% of time):** Answer everything you know instantly. Don't think — if the answer doesn't come within 30 seconds, mark it and move on.

**Pass 2 (40% of time):** Return to marked questions. Apply elimination, keyword recognition, and deeper thinking.

**Pass 3 (20% of time):** Review all answers. Focus on:
- Questions you changed your answer on (first instinct is usually right)
- "EXCEPT" / "NOT" questions (easy to misread)
- Any unanswered questions (guess if needed — never leave blank)

### 4.2 Time Allocation Per Question

For a standard exam with N questions and T minutes:
- **Average time per question:** T / N minutes
- **Easy questions:** Half the average time
- **Hard questions:** Double the average time, but never more
- **If stuck:** Mark it, move on, come back in Pass 2

**Quick reference (common exam lengths):**

| Exam Length | Total Time | Per Question | Easy | Hard |
|------------|-----------|-------------|------|------|
| 50 questions | 90 min | 1.8 min | 1 min | 3 min |
| 100 questions | 120 min | 1.2 min | 0.5 min | 2 min |
| 30 questions | 60 min | 2 min | 1 min | 4 min |

**中国考试补充：**

| 考试 | 题量 | 时间 | 每题时间 | 策略 |
|-----|------|------|---------|------|
| 公考行测 | 130-135题 | 120min | ~0.9min | 必须放弃10-15题 |
| 四六级阅读 | 30题 | 40min | ~1.3min | 先题干后文章 |
| 考研政治 | 38题(客观) | 180min(含主观) | ~2min | 选择题控制在60min内 |
| CPA单科 | ~50题 | 150min | ~3min | 客观题40min，主观题110min |

### 4.3 The "Panic Reset" (紧急重置)

When you're running out of time and still have many unanswered questions.

**Emergency protocol:**
1. Stop all current work
2. Go to unanswered questions
3. For each: spend exactly 15 seconds reading, then pick the "best guess" using probability guessing rules
4. Never leave a question blank — guessing has a 25% chance of being right (4-option)
5. If time permits after guessing all, go back to the hardest ones

## 5. Mindset & Exam Day

### 5.1 Pre-Exam Checklist (考前清单)

**The night before:**
- [ ] ID / admission ticket
- [ ] Pens, pencils, eraser (bring extras)
- [ ] Calculator (if allowed)
- [ ] Water bottle
- [ ] Watch (non-smart, if allowed)
- [ ] Review only high-frequency topics and your error notes — do NOT try to learn new material
- [ ] Sleep 7+ hours — sleep deprivation kills performance more than lack of knowledge

**30 minutes before exam:**
- Arrive early, find your seat, settle in
- Do a quick mental review of your top 5 most-missed topics
- Deep breathing: 4 seconds in, 7 seconds hold, 8 seconds out (repeat 3 times)

### 5.2 During-Exam Mindset (考场心态)

**When you encounter a question you don't know:**
1. This is expected — you prepared for speed, not perfection
2. Skip it immediately, come back later
3. When you return, use elimination — you only need to eliminate 2 options to have a 50% chance
4. Remember: you don't need 100%, you need to pass

**When anxiety hits:**
1. Put your pen down for 10 seconds
2. Take 3 deep breaths
3. Remind yourself: "I have tactics. I don't need to know everything."
4. Pick up the pen, start with the easiest question you can find

**When you finish early:**
1. Never leave early — use remaining time to check
2. Re-read "EXCEPT" and "NOT" questions first
3. Check answer sheet alignment (did you skip a line?)
4. Verify your name and ID are on the sheet

### 5.3 The "Don't Change" Rule

**Statistical truth:** Your first instinct is correct ~60-70% of the time.

**When to change:** Only when you find a clear, logical reason (e.g., you misread the question, you found new information in another question).

**When NOT to change:** When you're just feeling uncertain or second-guessing yourself.

### 5.4 The "Low Bar" Mindset (底线心态)

For cram-style preparation:

1. **Define your minimum passing score** — e.g., "I need 60/100"
2. **Calculate: how many questions can I afford to get wrong?** — e.g., "40 out of 100"
3. **This means: I can skip/be wrong on 40% and still pass**
4. **Frame every question as: "Is this in my 60% or my 40%?"**
5. This reframing dramatically reduces anxiety — wrong answers are BUILT INTO your strategy
